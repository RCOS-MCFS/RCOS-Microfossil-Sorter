What are microfossils?

Microfossils are bones (or bone fragments) 2-3 mm in size. They typically come from rodents, lizards, and other small vertebrates. Microfossils are commonly found in areas of geological or paleontological interest, and are gathered from most excavation sites in order to provide more information to scientists about past environments.

They can come in a variety of colors, and infinite variety of shapes depending on their initial state and their natural environment.

When harvested, microfossils are in a blend of gravel and fossils called microfossil concentrate.

Why are you sorting them?

Due to the abundance of microfossils and the information that they can reveal about past flora, fauna and geological events, they are potentially an important source of big data in paleontology, geology, stratigraphy, and other branches of science.

Due to the quantity of microfossil concentrate collected from sites, sorting concentrate is an incredibly long and tedious process, so concentrate often goes for many years without being sorted. Automating the sorting process would be very beneficial for paleontology, providing more complete data about locations studied and may lead to new research opportunities.

How are they sorted?

Due to the innumerable shapes and sizes of these fossils, many traditional computer vision techniques which are more based around recognizing certain shapes or patterns in objects are not valid options. Instead, we use color averages within detected edges, as well as some general features of this edge, as the discriminating values. While we have had success with this method, it does require the retraining of models when shifting to concentrate from a new location with different average colors. The steps from training to application are detailed below.

First in generating a new model, the user will need to adjust the sliders for the four parameters which determine the thresholding in order to assert that the best possible edge is being chosen.

With our edge parameters set, the user then takes manually labels the contents of the edges detected as rock or bone, with the r and b keys. The exact number required for best results has not yet been determined.

With the labeled images set, the SVM is then trained on a portion of this data. Once training is complete, the accuracy on the remainder of the data is reported, and the model is saved.



Using either a freshly generated or previously saved model, the software begins live classifying elements from the concentrate as they pass infront of the lens. While the hardware component of this project is still in development, it would direct the a servo motor at this point to deposit the sample into one of two containers, and catalog the numbers classified.

Where are you in the project?

At the moment, the software is largely complete. We are of course always keeping our ears open to new methods and ways in which we can improve this project, but presently all that remains is improving the UI for all tools so that it is as approachable as possible to members of the paleontology community who don't want to use command line utilities existing currently. While the current software runs admirably even on the extremely underpowered laptop used for most testing, our target platform is a Raspberry Pi, so it may be refined for greater optimization depending on the demands and capabilities of this final platform.

The hardware is still early in its development, though a prototype is expected to be completed by the end of the semester.
